About

The Experiment

Digital Fingers is a blind listening test. Every round plays a short piano clip, and you decide whether it came from human hands or from software reading the score. Then the site tells you the truth, and more usefully, why.

How the clips are made

The machine clips are rendered offline from public-domain typesettings of the scores, using the freely licensed Salamander Grand Piano samples. They come in three tiers of realism: deadpan (the score executed exactly, every note the same weight), humanized (small random timing and touch errors), and expressive (programmed rubato, phrase-shaped dynamics, voiced melodies, simulated pedal). The human clips are real recordings of real pianists, released into the public domain.

The violin section

Piano was the first pool, but pianos are also the easy case: a key only knows when it was pressed and how hard. A violin section runs the same blind test on an instrument where the human fingerprint lives in bowing, vibrato, and intonation instead. It has its own pool of clips, its own local score history, and its own crowd numbers, so the two experiments never mix. The violin pool is still being assembled — human performances and machine renders are vetted under the same provenance and loudness rules before any clip goes live.

Twins

Most pieces in the pool exist twice: once played by a human and once rendered by the machine. Same excerpt, different hands. A session never plays a piece more than once, and it picks which version you get at random, so on replay the same piece may switch sides. Recognizing the music tells you nothing. Only the playing does. Fourteen of the thirty-four pieces currently have both versions, from the Raindrop Prelude to Für Elise to Träumerei.

Fairness engineering

A listening test is only fair if the answer isn't leaked by the recording quality. Every clip on this site, human and machine alike, goes through the exact same processing chain: the same loudness level, the same fades, the same encoder, the same sample rate. No clip is louder, cleaner, or more produced than another. If you can tell them apart, it's because of the playing.

Your data

Almost none. Your scores and session history live in your own browser via localStorage. No analytics, no cookies, no accounts. The one exception: finished sessions add to anonymous running totals. A right/wrong counter for each clip (that's how the reveal can say how many players a clip has fooled), and if you answered the musical-training question, your session score joins your group's total so the results page can compare trained and untrained ears. These are plain counters on a server, with no identifiers of any kind attached. Nothing about you is stored, and nothing can be.

Colophon

Hand-built with plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No frameworks, no build step. The render pipeline is a small Node script that parses MIDI, applies an expression model, and mixes samples offline, so the site serves plain audio files and playback is identical on every device. Source pieces, tiers, and the full methodology are documented in the project README.
