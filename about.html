The Experiment

Digital Fingers is a blind listening test. Every round plays a short solo-piano excerpt; you decide whether it came from human hands or from software reading the score. Then the site tells you the truth, and — more usefully — why.

How the clips are made

The machine clips are rendered offline from public-domain typesettings of the scores, using the freely licensed Salamander Grand Piano samples. They come in three tiers of realism: deadpan (the score executed exactly, every note the same weight), humanized (small random timing and touch errors), and expressive (programmed rubato, phrase-shaped dynamics, voiced melodies, simulated pedal). The human clips are real recordings of real pianists, released into the public domain.

Twins

Several pieces exist in the pool twice: once played by a human, once rendered by the machine — the same excerpt, different hands. A session never plays a piece more than once, and it picks which version you get at random, so on replay the same prelude may switch sides. Recognizing the music tells you nothing; only the playing does. (Chopin's Preludes Op. 28 Nos. 4, 6, and 7 are currently twinned; more pairs will join as verifiably public-domain recordings and typesettings line up.)

Fairness engineering

A listening test is only interesting if the answer isn't leaked by the plumbing. Every clip on this site — human and machine alike — exits through an identical processing chain: loudness normalized to −16 LUFS with a static gain, the same fade shapes, the same encoder at the same quality, the same sample rate. No clip is louder, cleaner, or more "produced" than another. If you can tell them apart, it's because of the playing.

Audio credits & licensing

Everything you hear is legally in the public domain — the compositions, the recordings, and the samples.

  • Human performances — from Musopen's Complete Chopin Collection, recordings commissioned by Musopen and dedicated to the public domain (CC0): the Nocturnes Op. 9 No. 2, Op. 15 No. 1, and Op. 27 No. 1; the Mazurkas Op. 17 No. 4 and Op. 24 No. 2; and the Preludes Op. 28 Nos. 1, 3, and 6. Also from Musopen: Beethoven's Moonlight Sonata (third movement) performed by Paul Pitman, and Chopin's Waltz Op. 69 No. 1 performed by Luke Faulkner — both public domain. The Goldberg Variations Aria is performed by Kimiko Ishizaka, from the Open Goldberg Variations (CC0). Excerpts were trimmed and loudness-normalized; the performances are untouched.
  • Machine renderings — generated for this site from note data in Mutopia Project typesettings (all marked public domain by their typesetters): Bach's Prelude in C BWV 846; Petzold's Minuets BWV Anh. 114 and 115; Beethoven's Für Elise; Chopin's Preludes Op. 28 Nos. 4 and 7; Satie's Gymnopédie No. 1; Mozart's Fantasia in D minor K. 397; Schumann's Kinderszenen Nos. 1 and 7 (Träumerei); Brahms's Waltz Op. 39 No. 15 and Intermezzo Op. 118 No. 2; Scriabin's Prelude Op. 11 No. 1; and Debussy's Clair de lune and First Arabesque.
  • Piano samples — the Salamander Grand Piano by Alexander Holm (Creative Commons Attribution 3.0), a Yamaha C5 sampled at Musikhögskolan i Piteå.
  • Type — Cormorant Garamond by Christian Thalmann (SIL Open Font License), self-hosted.

All compositions are by composers who died well over a century ago and are public domain worldwide. The Musopen recordings carry a CC0 dedication, which waives performer and producer rights. If you believe any attribution here is incomplete, please get in touch and it will be corrected.

Your data

There isn't any. Scores and session history are stored in your own browser via localStorage and never leave your device. No analytics, no cookies, no accounts.

Colophon

Hand-built with vanilla HTML, CSS, and JavaScript — no frameworks, no build step. The render pipeline is a small Node script that parses MIDI, applies an expression model, and mixes samples offline, so the site serves plain audio files and playback is identical on every device. Source pieces, tiers, and the full methodology are documented in the project README.
