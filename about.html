About

The Experiment

Digital Fingers is a blind listening test. Choose piano or violin, hear a short clip, and decide whether it came from human bodies or from software reading the score. Then the site tells you the truth, and more usefully, why.

How the clips are made

The machine clips are rendered offline from licensed score and performance data. Piano uses the freely licensed Salamander Grand samples and three tiers of realism: deadpan (the score executed exactly), humanized (small timing and touch variations), and expressive (programmed rubato, phrase-shaped dynamics, voicing, and pedal). Violin uses licensed Mutopia scores rendered through FluidSynth and GeneralUser GS strings. The human clips are real performances shared under public-domain or Creative Commons terms.

Twins

Every piece exists twice: once played by humans and once rendered by the machine. The piano pool has 38 paired pieces; the violin pool has eight, spanning Vivaldi, Bach, Schubert, and Beethoven. A session never plays a piece more than once, and it picks which version you get at random, so on replay the same music may switch sides. Recognizing it tells you nothing. Only the performance does.

Fairness engineering

A listening test is only fair if the answer isn't leaked by the recording quality. Every clip on this site, human and machine alike, goes through the exact same processing chain: the same loudness level, the same fades, the same encoder, the same sample rate. No clip is louder, cleaner, or more produced than another. If you can tell them apart, it's because of the playing.

How difficulty adapts

Every clip in the game carries a rating, and so do you. Both start at 1500, the number chess gives a newcomer. Each round is a tiny match: call the clip correctly and you win rating points from it; get fooled and it takes points from you. The exchange is bigger when the upset is bigger — beating an easy clip earns almost nothing, while catching a famously deceptive render pays well. Early judgments move ratings quickly so new clips find their level fast; then the steps shrink and everything settles. Your rating never leaves your browser, like the rest of your history. The clips’ ratings live with the other anonymous counters. Sessions use them to serve a spread: a couple of clips near your level, one softer, one harder, and always at least one expressive render. Difficulty belongs to the piece, never to the side you happen to get, so a clip’s rating can’t whisper the answer.

Accessibility

The whole game plays from the keyboard: Space plays the clip, H and M answer, Enter moves on. Screen readers hear each round and reveal announced as it happens, the Learn page's demos say which version they're playing, and every animation stands still if your system asks for reduced motion. One limit we won't paper over: this is a test of hearing, and without audio there is no game — so the site isn't accessible to deaf visitors, and pretending otherwise would help no one. If something here doesn't work for you and should, please get in touch and it will be fixed.

Your data

Almost none. Your scores and session history live in your own browser via localStorage. No analytics, no cookies, no accounts. The one exception: finished sessions add to anonymous running totals. A right/wrong counter for each clip (that's how the reveal can say how many players a clip has fooled), and if you answered the musical-training question, your session score joins your group's total so the end-of-session chart and the public results page can compare trained and untrained ears. These are plain counters on a server, with no identifiers of any kind attached. Nothing about you is stored, and nothing can be.

Colophon

Hand-built with plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No frameworks, no build step. The piano pipeline parses MIDI, applies an expression model, and mixes samples offline; the violin pipeline renders licensed ensemble scores through a General MIDI soundfont. Both finish with the same trim, fade, loudness, sample-rate, and encoding chain, so the site serves plain audio files and playback is identical on every device. Source pieces, tiers, and the full methodology are documented in the project README.

The Learn page's expression mixer is sixteen pre-rendered combinations of four expression layers — no live audio processing in the browser; each switch simply crossfades to the matching file, loudness-matched like everything else, and cached for offline like the rest of the pool.

The site is also an installable app that works offline. A small hand-written service worker keeps the pages, type, and artwork on your device, quietly saves enough clip pairs for a full session while you're connected, and holds any finished session's anonymous counters until the connection returns. Same rules offline as on: a piece plays once, and which side you get stays a coin flip.
