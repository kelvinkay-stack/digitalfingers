How to Hear a Human

A piano key doesn't know who pressed it. A hammer hits a string at some speed, at some moment, and that's all the instrument ever knows. Everything we call expression comes down to two numbers, when and how hard, repeated a few thousand times. So everything human about a performance is hiding in those two numbers. Here's where to listen for it.

Each section below ends with two recordings of the same eight bars of Satie's first Gymnopédie. The first is a deadpan render, meaning the score played exactly as written with nothing added. The second adds back one human skill, and only that one. Listen to them back to back and you'll hear what each skill actually does.

i. Micro-timing and rubato

Nobody plays perfectly in time. Not even professionals who think they do. Measure a real pianist against a grid and their notes land early and late by small amounts, but the misses aren't random. A player leans into the start of a phrase, pushes through the middle, and relaxes at the end, the same way a sentence has a shape when you say it out loud. Musicians call this rubato, which means stolen time. You steal a little here and pay it back there.

Computers fail at this in a funny way. Software can shake up the timing randomly, and the result sounds like a nervous robot, not a person. That's because human timing errors point somewhere. So when you're deciding human or machine, don't ask whether the timing is imperfect. Ask whether the imperfections are going anywhere.

ii. Dynamic phrasing and voicing

Hit every note at the same volume and a piano turns into a typewriter. Real players shape volume at two levels at once. Across a phrase, the sound rises and falls in an arc, swelling toward the note that matters and settling after it. And inside a single chord, a pianist plays the melody note a little stronger than its neighbors so the tune stands out from the background. That trick is called voicing.

Voicing is the harder thing to fake, because it means knowing which note matters, and that's a musical judgment, not a math problem. If a recording gets louder and softer but still feels strangely flat, this is usually why. Everything is getting louder and softer together, and no single line ever steps forward.

iii. The pedal and its haze

The sustain pedal is the piano's breath. Press it and the dampers lift off every string, so strings you never played start humming along with the ones you did. A real performance carries that haze. The sound builds up, blurs, and then clears when the player changes the pedal, usually a split second after the harmony changes, because it sounds better when the new chord blooms into the old one's fade.

Renders usually treat the pedal like a light switch flipped exactly on the beat. Clean, punctual, and airless. Listen for the space around the notes. If every chord sits in its own sealed room, be suspicious. There's also plain old noise. A real pedal thumps, felt shifts, dampers brush against strings. Total silence between notes is a clue all by itself.

iv. Articulation, the space between notes

Two notes can be the same length on paper and feel completely different depending on how they end. A pianist lets some notes go early so there's daylight between them, melts others into the next one, and clips repeated notes so they bounce. Melodies usually get the connected, singing treatment while the accompaniment stays lighter and more detached, so the texture has layers.

A quantized render holds every note for exactly its written length. The effect is subtle but it's everywhere. The music feels laminated, with each note the same distance from its neighbors. Once you hear it, you can't unhear it.

v. Imperfection as signature

Here's the uncomfortable part: everything above can be programmed. Rubato curves, volume arcs, voicing, pedal models. The renders in this game use all of them, and the good ones are genuinely hard to catch. What still gives the machine away isn't a lack of imperfection. It's a lack of risk. A live performance is a body solving a physical problem in real time. Fingers travel, hands have habits, and the fourth finger is weaker than the second and always will be. Human flaws form a consistent personal pattern, like a signature. Machine flaws form a statistic.

So the final skill isn't spotting mistakes. It's asking whether the performance sounds like it could have gone differently. Whether anything was ever at stake. Perfection can be rendered. Almost losing it, so far, can't.

Deadpan against everything at once

Why this matters

Machine-made music isn't a curiosity anymore. It's a growing share of everything you hear, and the gap this site trains you to notice is the exact gap the technology is working to close. Modern systems don't just shake up the timing. They learn phrasing and voicing from thousands of hours of human playing, which means they're learning to imitate intention itself.

It's worth being clear about what's actually at stake, because it isn't "machines can't be musical." Expression is information. When a pianist slows down at the end of a phrase, they're telling you something about how the harmony resolves. When they bring out an inner line, they're showing you a structure you'd otherwise miss. A performance is an argument about a piece. What the machine gives you, so far, is the conclusion without the argument.

Whether that difference stays audible is an open question, and your ears are the experiment. The honest answer from this site: the deadpan renders fool nobody, and the expressive ones fool almost everyone at least once. The question is getting harder. That's not a reason to panic, but it is a reason to know what close listening feels like while the difference is still there to hear.
